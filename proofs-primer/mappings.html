Bijections in math

Bijections between sets is not the most useful thing. However, after adding on some structure to the sets, i.e. extra requirements that the set must satisfy, the concept of bijections transforms into the idea of isomorphisms. For example, in group theory, instead of considering bijections between two groups, we consider homomorphisms , which must satisfy the constraint that . In turn, two groups are isomorphic iff there exists a bijective homomorphism between them. Similarly, in linear algebra, two vectorspaces are isomorphic iff there exists a bijective linear mapping between them. The point to note here is that not all functions are linear mappings, and not all functions are homomorphisms, and so it is harder to construct these functions.

Yet, due to these additional restrictions required to confirm isomorphisms between vectorspaces and groups, isomorphisms allow one to think of a vectorspace in terms of another, or a group in terms of another. In fact, linear algebra is powerful because of its use of isomorphisms to represent many abstract vectorspaces by vectors or matrices of real numbers. Bijections in organizing thoughts

Consider having two lists. If every element on one list corresponds with exactly one on another, and the lists are the same size, and every element on the second list is mapped to, then there is a bijection between the lists.

In general, mappings sometimes unravel thought patterns. For example, listing thoughts on one side, moods on another side, and drawing lines to connect them may establish what moods you experience often. However, the thoughts on the left side could vary depending on the moment, so the mapping might also vary depending on the moment.

The end

This is the end of the primer. What comes next depends on your goals:

  • If you are working on self-analysis, then journalling more often helps. Use implications to help identify thought patterns.
  • If you are going into math, then read up a basic linear algebra textbook. It will likely start immediately with proofs, but since you have an idea of the types of proofs now, you won’t get as lost.
  • If you are going into pure math, then after revising on linear algebra, consider reading up on abstract algebra and analysis.

Good luck. But more importantly, never prove by assuming and showing follows from that assumption!
