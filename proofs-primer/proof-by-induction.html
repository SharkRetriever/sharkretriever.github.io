A line of dominos

Suppose on your table, you set up a line of fifty standing dominos. What does it take for all of them to tip over, assuming you don’t just flip the table? There are two requirements:

  • There exists an initial domino that tips over. In other words, you have a “starting domino” that you can push over.
  • For every domino that tips over (including the initial domino), the next domino must also tip over.

With these two requirements satisfied, we can push a domino on one end of the line (the initial domino), and that domino must push over the next domino in line, by the second requirement. However, the second requirement now repeats on the second domino, and so the second domino must push over the third domino, and this continues until the last domino tips over.

Notice that no end point is specified. We do not actually need the “line” of dominos to have an ending point, but rather just a starting point. As long as the second property holds, after the first domino is tipped, any domino in line will fall.

Induction on false statements

Attempting to use induction on false statements is dangerous. If you attempt to prove a false statement by induction, then one or more of the following might happen:

  • The base case does not hold
  • The inductive hypothesis does not always imply the inductive conclusion (this is especially an issue for small , such as this case)

Debugging induction

Most induction questions are fairly simple, because they follow the pattern above. Just set up the base case, and make sure the inductive hypothesis implies the inductive conclusion. If the induction question is difficult, it could be for the following reasons:

  • The question is not meant to be solved by induction. Try using another proof method that is more direct.
  • The statement might be true only for odd or even numbers. Try showing instead.
  • The statement might be true only for powers of two. Rewrite as and use induction on . This is a common pattern in CS.
  • The inductive conclusion might need more information than just the inductive hypothesis. Consider splitting this part of the proof into cases, and analyzing each case.

If none of this advice helped, then attempt to speak with a professor, classmate, or friend about your induction issues. (If you got here while trying to search up how to fix an induction cooktop, you’re on the wrong site.)
