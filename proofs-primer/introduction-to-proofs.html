A Primer on Proofs – Introduction

This section of the site introduces theorem-proving techniques to high school students and curious non-math majors. Immediately, a question that comes to mind is:

Why would you ever need proofs?

Well,

  • If you are a high-school student planning to go into math or computer science (CS), then expect at least one proof to show up in every math course you take.
  • If you are a high-school student planning to go into computer or software engineering, then expect at least one course involving proofs to show up (probably in linear algebra).

These reasons are more of an application of scare tactics though, so here’s a “better” one:

  • Proofs certify the validity of a theorem. In a non-mathematical context, proofs help you think more critically about your ideas and thoughts.

Mathematicians come up with and prove theorems. These theorems then make their way through other sciences, and eventually go into industry in the form of goods and services. For example, SSDs wouldn’t exist if it weren’t for quantum tunneling. Yet, quantum theory wouldn’t work as reliably if it weren’t for calculus, followed by advancements in differential equations and group theory.

Proofs for self-reflection and self-analysis

Later on, we will see that many proofs rely on “if-then” statements, called implications. When attempting to trace trains of thought, implications can help make clear the cause of a problem or opinion. Re-phrasing opinions in the form of implications might also help catch logical fallacies that others, or you yourself, commit.

Personally, I have used implications before to trace some self-critical thoughts to sources of external pressure with success.

(Note: if you’re here to learn proofs in a context outside of mathematics, proceed to Boolean algebra, which introduces the concept of a logical statement, and a few ways to manipulate them.)

Thus, even though testing cannot cover all cases (otherwise the product would probably not be delivered on time), testing dilligently is better than not testing at all.

At least for math and computer science, researchers must prove all of their original results, since any engineer or developer who uses said result must know exactly when it works and when it doesn’t. Meanwhile in software development, the developer usually considers a sample of all possible cases that a user may encounter, and that’s about it.

However, that doesn’t mean developers necessarily get a free pass. Firstly, testing can be quite tedious at times, for developers must still consider as many edge cases as they can think up.

Secondly, there are consequences for not testing thoroughly enough. For example, computers often, but not always, store numbers in a fixed number of bits. Thus, if I used 64 bits of storage to store a large decimal number, and then tried to convert it into an integer that only has 16 bits of storage, then something will probably go wrong, due to the 48-bit reduction in storage space. Yet, instructions to do exactly that within the software of the Ariane 5 rocket are what caused it to explode.
