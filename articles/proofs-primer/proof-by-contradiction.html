Proof by Contradiction

Proving things by contradiction is a great feeling. You assume the negation is true, and then show that something breaks. Then, you go “Ha! See? There’s no way the negation is true, so the original statement must be true.”

We start off with some new terminology needed for this section. Then, we demonstrate an example proof, and explain that proofs by contradiction show up frequently in reality.

Proving by contradiction in real life

Proving a contradiction in real life is equivalent to detecting lies, or picking a side.

Hypothetically, if it’s now evening, and your roommate says they will be studying for the whole evening, but then you hear them screaming over a game of LoL two minutes later, then the statement “My roommate is studying for the whole evening” is false, because there is evidence that your roommate isn’t studying for the whole evening. Accepting both their original claim and the current evidence results in a contradiction, leading you to throw out the case that is more unlikely, which in this case is their claim.

For me, cognitive dissonance also arises from seeing or hearing contradicting beliefs within a short gap of time from two different people, or even from the same person. Sometimes, I might state contradictory thoughts while freewriting on a page, such as “I think having as a hobby is so cool… is so lame, though”. In this case, if I can catch myself writing down such a contradiction, then I can think about which opinion resonates more with me, and pick that one. I can also try to track down the inspiration for each side. For example, my parents might believe in one opinion, but my friends might believe in another. Then, the final opinion comes down to whom I agree with more.
