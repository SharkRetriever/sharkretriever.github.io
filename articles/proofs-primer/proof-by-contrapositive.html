Notice that we are starting to cite other theorems while proving new theorems. This is what mathematicians do all day in research.

But, who would ever come up with such a negated statement to begin with?

In math, the negation of a property is often given a name as well. Thus, if a natural number is not prime, it’s composite. If a class in a Markov chain is not closed, it’s open. Thus, some theorems might be phrased in a way that makes it easier to prove when the contrapositive is used to flip the statement around, even if the original statement is clear to begin with.

When it comes to reflecting on thoughts in general, ideas could come out in a way where both the hypothesis and conclusion are negated for effect. For example, a thought I might have is “If the A/C didn’t break at home, then I wouldn’t be at the library right now”. Now, we can use the contrapositive on this thought, and change it into “If I am at the library, then the A/C broke at home”. This statement implies that being at the library means that the A/C must have broke at home. However, if the hypothesis is false, meaning I am not at the library, then we cannot conclude whether or not the A/C is broken at home. To review this fact, revisit the page on implications.

Otherwise, it’s time to glue your bottom to the seat while we look over a technique used more often after your first proofs course, proof by contradiction. (Hint: there’s a reason why I specifically used the word “bottom”).
