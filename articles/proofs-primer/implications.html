Implications

Implications are the bread and butter of proofs. Given two statements and , an implication, denoted , means that “ implies ”, or equivalently, “If , then ”. What does it mean for an implication to be true?

The implication is true as long as it doesn’t “lie”. That is, if the implication is truthful, then if the hypothesis is true, the conclusion must follow. However, if the hypothesis is not true, the implication does not give us any conclusion. However, this does not mean that the implication “lied” to us, and so we say the implication is vacuously true.

The concept of “vacuous truth” seems incredibly arbitrary, and usually, we can assume that the hypothesis must be true. However, we will see some implications later on where we can check for this case, and reflect on how it changes the meaning of the statement, especially in the biconditionals section.

What does it mean for an implication to be false?

By the section above, an implication is false when the hypothesis is true but the conclusion is false. In this case, the hypothesis does not imply the conclusion, and so the implication is false.

The purpose of implications in math

Implications are pipes that the plumber, I mean mathematician, uses to show one truth leads to another. In math, the way to obtain new results is to build on old results. Thus, instead of showing something is true starting from nothing every time, the mathematician can take results from previous papers, and fuse the results into a proof for a new result.

Likewise, undergraduate students studying mathematics are often given a set of powerful theorems in class, which they then use to prove more theorems that appear on assignments. In my PMATH 336 course (group theory for non-pure-math majors), I even had to look back at previous assignment questions from time to time to use those theorems, that I proved, to help me prove yet another implication or statement on a later assignment. Thus, implications really do become building blocks of mathematical truth.

Implications in everyday life

In real life, implications demonstrate actions that could happen, given that a hypothesis turns out to be true. For example, “if it rains, then I will not go outside” is such an implication. “If you lift every other day and eat at a Calorie surplus, then you will develop more muscles” is another such implication, although, this implication is true “to more people” than the previous one, because the hypothesis in this case usually causes the conclusion, whereas the previous implication indicates a decision that someone else might not make. Notice that for these examples, we cannot say that the hypothesis will lead to the conclusion anymore, because everyone is different, and so an implication that applies to one person might not apply to another. This discrepancy between math and “reality” is explained on the next page.

Implications can also eliminate possibilities. For example, “if the notebook is red, then it is the one I want for this course” helps when you see two notebooks, one red, and one green, in your backpack, and need to know which one to pull out. Notice that technically, the green notebook makes the hypothesis false, which makes the statement vacuously true, meaning that the conclusion becomes inconclusive; the green notebook might also be the notebook that you want for this course. To toughen up the statement, add another implication: “if the notebook is not red, then it is not the one I want for this course.”

Implications in self-analysis

One way I use implications, is to analyze thoughts from a negative inner critical voice. For example, take the statement “I’m a loser because I didn’t get wasted last weekend.” Now that we know implications, we can rephrase the self-accusation as follows:

  • If I did not get wasted last weekend, then I am a loser.

Since a negative thought like this doesn’t exactly help me out, I can either try to “disprove” the implication by finding a counterexample, or try to “prove” it within my point of view, and see where the proof falls apart by going into someone else’s point of view. To disprove it, I must find a counterexample to the implication; I need to find someone who does not go and get wasted on weekends, but who is not a loser from their point of view. Disproving this implication then equates to finding someone who believes the implication is false. In effect, if you use this trick, you might just end up finding someone supportive of your thought, which is comfy.

To “prove” the implication, I have to show that not getting wasted leads to having some other personality trait, or doing some other action instead, that leads me to being a loser in my frame of reference. I lead on the reasoning with implications, and assuming that the hypothesis is true, attempt to derive the conclusion.

For example, I can write “I did not get wasted last weekend” “I decided not to go to any parties that weekend” “I finished some assignments instead” “I was a loser”.

From this line of reasoning, we notice that the last jump seems unwarranted, and that the opinion expressed by the inner critical voice seems to come from people in the past other than myself. Realizing the source of the implication, I can then think about who suggested (either verbally or non-verbally) this implication to me, and whether or not I should consider taking it seriously in the future.

Anyway, while I go and contact a few people, and ask them whether or not they got wasted last weekend, and whether or not they consider themselves a loser, continue onwards to learning about the importance of definitions.
