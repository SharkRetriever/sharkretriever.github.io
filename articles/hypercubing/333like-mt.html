Exploring 3x3x3-Like Puzzles (Part I – Orientability)

Written April 10, 2020.

Abstract

In this article, we first define regular 3x3x3-like puzzles (regular 333LPs), which generalize the 3x3x3 and the Megaminx. We then explore several 333LPs embedded on different surfaces using Roice Nelson’s MagicTile program. We note how the solving experience changes for the different puzzles depending on their orientability and their size, and in the end, list off regular 333LPs most similar to the 3x3x3 and the Megaminx.

TL;DR: The second paragraph of the Conclusion section at the end of this article lists out the puzzles that behave most similarly to the 3x3x3 and the Megaminx.

Regular 3x3x3-Like Puzzles

We define a regular 3x3x3-like puzzle (a regular 333LP) as any twisty puzzle similar enough to a 3x3x3 to be solved like a 3x3x3 using an edges-first corners-second approach, or in some cases, even CFOP. In other words, 333LPs generalize the 3x3x3 and the Megaminx. Almost all the puzzles presented in this article can first be solved by intuitively placing the edges, and then using variations on [[R’, F], D] and/or [[R: U], D] to solve the corners.

Surfaces and Orientability

We use Roice Nelson’s MagicTile program to try out 333LPs on various surfaces. In this article, we try regular 333LPs on the sphere, the projective plane, the torus, the Klein bottle, and the hyperbolic plane.

A feature that comes up often when exploring these various surfaces is orientability. We say a surface is non-orientable if a 2D figure can be placed on it at some starting point, and then moved around the surface in such a way that the figure arrives back at the starting point mirror-reversed. Examples of non-orientable surfaces include the Möbius strip and Klein bottle. Orientable surfaces are thus the opposite of non-orientable surfaces. For orientable surfaces, 2D figures placed on some starting point cannot come back to that same point mirror-reversed.

Twisting Single Corners on Non-Orientable Puzzles

To twist a single corner on non-orientable puzzles, we can first twist two corners. Using a commutator such as [[R’, F]2, D] or [[R, U]2, D], one corner will be twisted clockwise, and the other counterclockwise. Then, we can bring one of the corners to the “opposite” side of the puzzle, which mirrors it due to non-orientability. We can then untwist the two corners with the same commutator, leaving only one of the corners twisted after undoing the setup moves.

The image below shows a single corner twisted on the 9-coloured Klein bottle puzzle.

Note that a single corner cannot be twisted on orientable 333LPs since the corner orientation of a single corner is dependent on that of all the other corners, meaning if all but the last corner is solved, then the last corner must be solved too (unless the puzzle is in an illegal/unsolvable permutation).

Tilings and Schläfli Symbols

We use the Schläfli symbol to denote the various tilings explored in this article. The Schläfli symbol represents a tiling with -gons per vertex, where a -gon is a -sided polygon. It turns out that all 333LPs require to obtain 3x3x3-like cuts, so we can focus merely on tilings instead.

Examples of 333LPs

The Sphere

The sphere is an orientable surface. We can tile the sphere with tilings for . When we obtain a tetrahedron, when we obtain a cube, and when we obtain a dodecahedron. However, we can discard the case since the face-turning tetrahedron is Jing’s Pyraminx, which is not solved in the same way as the 3x3x3 or the Megaminx.

Therefore, the spherical regular 333LPs are exactly the 3x3x3 (shown below, left) and the Megaminx (shown below, right). However, they appear different in this program due to the use of stereographic projection.

Considering that these 333LPs are the only ones that can be physically produced, we can use these puzzles as a reference point for the other 333LPs below. For the purpose of this article, we claim a 333LP is “not limited in size” if both [[R’, F], D] and [[R: U], D] can be executed from all angles, and only cycle three corners each time. Otherwise, we claim that the 333LP is “limited in size”.

Both the 3x3x3 and the Megaminx are not limited in size by the definition above.

The Projective Plane

The projective plane is a non-orientable surface that can be thought of as a sphere where opposite points are the exact same point. For example, whereas we can project a cube and dodecahedron onto a sphere, we can project a hemicube and a hemidodecahedron onto the projective plane. The hemicube is an abstract polyhedron with three faces, but we can represent it as a cube where opposite edges are the same edge, opposite vertices are the same vertex, and opposite faces are the same face. The hemidodecahedron and dodecahedron relate to each other in a similar way.

Then, based on the hemicube and the hemidodecahedron, we can construct the 3x3x3 hemicube (shown below, left) and the Hemimegaminx (shown below, right). In MagicTile, these puzzles are represented as spherical puzzles where opposite faces are the same face. Thus, if one face is rotated clockwise, the opposite face rotates counterclockwise simultaneously. Shown below are the 3x3x3 hemicube and the Hemimegaminx.

The 3x3x3 hemicube is limited in size but trivial, for it can be boiled down into a three-colour slice-turning 3x3x3. Therefore, the corners are always solved, and a solving strategy becomes solving the centers after solving the edges, which makes this puzzle an outlier relative to the other 333LPs.

Meanwhile, the Hemimegaminx is non-trivial. It can be solved with an edges-first approach, where the corners are afterwards solved using the commutator [[R’, F], D] but not [[R: U], D]; the latter does not work on the Hemimegaminx because it is limited in size. The Hemimegaminx can also be left with one twisted corner at the end of a solve because it is non-orientable.

The Torus

On the torus, solving the regular 333LPs, which take the form of tilings, is fairly straightforward for the most part. Note that we can represent puzzles on the torus by unfolding and stretching the torus into a flat torus.

For the 7-coloured torus 333LP (shown below, left), after the edges are solved, the corners can be placed only using [[R’, F], D], because the puzzle is limited in size. The 16-coloured torus 333LP (shown below, right), however, is not limited in size, and can be solved in the same way as the Megaminx.

The 6-coloured torus 333LP (shown below), meanwhile, is so limited in size that the only way to really place corners on such a puzzle after the edges are solved is to use careful applications of [[R: U], D].

The Klein Bottle

The Klein bottle is non-orientable, so once again it is possible to leave only one corner unsolved. We can represent it in a flat plane in a similar way to the flat torus above. On the 9-coloured Klein bottle, both [[R’, F], D] and [[R: U], D] can be used, although the latter cannot be used from all execution angles because the puzzle is limited in size.

Conclusions

3x3x3-like puzzles (333LPs) change in solving properties depending on their orientability and puzzle size.

The orientable 333LPs that are not limited in size and are viewable with MagicTile are: the 3x3x3, the Megaminx, the 16-coloured torus, the 24-coloured , the 24-coloured , and the 36-coloured . Since these puzzles are not limited in size, they can all be solved with the exact same edge-first corner-second method, i.e. solving the edges intuitively, and then applying either [[R’, F], D] or [[R: U], D] commutators to solve the corners.

Other 333LPs mentioned were either limited in size, such as the 12-coloured ; non-orientable, such as the 18-coloured ; or both, such as the 9-coloured Klein bottle and the Hemimegaminx. We also looked at the 3x3x3 hemicube, which is both non-orientable and limited in size, yet trivial. Thus, we write the following statements without taking the 3x3x3 hemicube into account. Non-orientable 333LPs can have twisted or mirrored corners, which increases the difficulty of the solve. 333LPs that are limited in size do not allow execution of the [[R’, F], D] and [[R: U], D] commutators from all possible execution angles, which also increases the difficulty of the solve.
