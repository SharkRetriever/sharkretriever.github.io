About Hypercubing

I was going to boldly state my take on hypercubing in the form of a concise definition…I couldn’t.

In general, hypercubing’s an activity somewhat related to speedcubing, except the puzzles exist in n-dimensional space, where n is “greater than 3”.
I wrap “greater than three” in quotes because there are exceptions. Essentially, I count these as examples of hypercubing:

  • Solving 4D puzzles
  • Solving puzzles that exist above 4-dimensional space, such as the 5D 3x3x3x3x3
  • Solving hyperbolic honeycombs (which exist in hyperbolic 3-dimensional space)

Really, the hardest part of hypercubing is ignoring the fact that the puzzle can’t exist perfectly in our world, and just solving it using commutators or other methods like any other 3D twisty puzzle you know. Now to add more details to each example I noted above:

Solving 4D Puzzles

The 4D hypercube is a great start into hypercubing, and I recommend, with full bias, MagicCube4D. The site even comes with a solution guide, although I also helped make one (here) for those who know how to solve using CFOP on the normal 3D cube.
Hence, if you know CFOP, you’re already halfway there. Note that to be more concise, I write 3x3x3 as 3^3 and 3x3x3x3 as 3^4 using exponential notation.

However, there’s another entire family in 4D that doesn’t exist in 3D called duoprisms. To avoid making things mathematically heavy for now, I’ll just say that they look like two intersecting donuts connected with each other at every point along their surfaces. However, turning them is somewhat similar to turning a 3x3x2 due to the symmetry of the puzzle, so duoprisms are a bit like fun 3x3x2s. If you want a clearer and more detailed definition, look here. Unfortunately, I haven’t found a solution for these yet, but I hope to do so. Considering how it’s an infinite family of puzzles, I feel as though it would be nice progress.

As for other 4D puzzles, there’s always Magic Puzzle Ultimate. Some of these puzzles include the 4D version of the Dino Cube, Skewb, and Helicopter cube. There’s even a non-shape-shifting 2x3x3x3, so there’s lots more to solve if for any reason you’re ever bored of just plain face-turning hypercubes. Oh also, there’s the 24-cell.

Lastly, if you have absolutely no life and are willing to spend a whole month or two solving the 4D analogue of the megaminx, have fun beating my fewest-moves record of around 20 thousand moves.

Solving Puzzles above 4D

Just like solving 4D puzzles, the hardest part of solving 5D+ puzzles is trying to understand how they turn. There are a few programs for this, and I recommend using either MagicCube5D or MagicCube7D. Of course, Magic Puzzle Ultimate also has 5D+ puzzles, but I don’t even want to look at them (how does the {3,3}^2 work anyway???)

Also, it’s impossible to construct a megaminx in over 4 dimensions, so we’re saved from more tedium in this regard.

Solving Hyperbolic Honeycombs

What is generally true is that solving 2D infinite tilings feels like solving 3D puzzles. For example, to solve the face-turning hexagonal tiling, you just have to know how to solve the 3^3. If you want to play with these infinite tilings, look here.

Likewise, solving 3D infinite tilings feels like solving 4D puzzles, hence why I considered it hypercubing. So far, I haven’t solved a single one of these types of puzzles. One example would be Magic 3D Hyperbolic Tile {6,3,3}. Essentially, this puzzle lives in 3-dimensional hyperbolic space. Each cell of the puzzle is a hexagonal tiling, and three cells meet at each edge. That’s right, unlike the 3^4 where each cell is just a boring cube, these cells are infinite. There’s nothing more to say – we should all just listen to the soft sound of mindblown instead.
