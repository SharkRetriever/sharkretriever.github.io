[Life] The Numbers Game, and how to fail at all your New Years' resolutions

Dec 18, 2017

(Last time I said I was going to talk about toilet paper usage rates in my dorm…let’s not, since there’s too many variables to consider.)

So, where have I been this time?

Studying at school and finishing assignments one after another.

The exam season is almost over and there remains one easy course. This year, I have made several resolutions and I have achieved none of them: I did not solve a 4x4x4 blindfolded, I did not read through a math paper every two months on some subject such as sequent calculus or complex analysis, I did not improve my sketching skills, and I did not finish implementing any of my side projects, nor did I refactor older side projects.

Then, the question becomes what went wrong, and what I can do for next year. Here is an analysis:

Forcing interests

This June, I tried to get into machine learning through taking the Stanford Machine Learning Course on Coursera. Even though the course was greatly simplified, meaning the concepts were easy to take in, I only realized until after I had finished the course that I did not exactly find the course a valuable use of my time, especially since I still do not have any machine learning side projects going on right now, nor do I have any planned for the next few months.

Thus, I could have worked on reading up on other material instead.

Speaking of other material, I did at least realize last month that I didn’t really need to know what a dual space was, so I stopped trying to shove that bit of linear algebra into my head, and started concentrating on other fields of math instead such as groups and rings, which I really need to at least build a foundation on.

Managing time improperly

I also noticed that even when given more time, I would often spend it on instant messaging services such as Messenger and Discord, or even worse, browsing YouTube. I realize that one reason why I am often surprised when I complete a 2.5-hour exam an hour early (when I do, that is) is because when I am at home and sitting at the computer blasting metal and talking about the ZZ method on a 3x3x3 instead, I don’t finish questions nearly as quickly.

In fact, this one time I finished eight self-assigned STAT 231 questions within 40 minutes just by closing the laptop lid and not listening to music.

Thus, the solution here is simply to schedule my screen time properly, and in terms of chatting, actually see how much of the chat content is useful. Of course, “useful” depends on whom I’m talking to.

Another solution which I am using right now is to use LeechBlock with a 60-second delay screen on YouTube, so I have to wait 60 seconds for the delay screen to count down (switching to another tab or window resets the timer), so I can go click on a video link and wait another 60 seconds until I can watch the video, and so on. This way, I think more about whether the wait is actually worth it, and I either close the tab, or I leave for a while to stretch and get my pencil case or scrap paper so that I can pressure myself into doing more productive things.

Failing to budget

This doesn’t really have to do with my resolutions, but wouldn’t it be silly if I said I didn’t know how much I was spending each month? Well, I don’t.

Because I do not know how much I am spending per month, I cannot keep track of my assets and cannot tell whether I am spending too little or too much. Then, because of impulsive shopping habits, I may end up going online shopping, or walking to the on-campus stationery store.

Thus, with a budget, I can track my expenditures and actually decide how to spend my remaining savings for different purposes such as investments, gifts, and travel, and if I can tell that I do not have enough to spend on extra materials, then I give myself incentive to not go “window” shopping, where “window” is in quotation marks because I usually end up buying things. In turn, I would also save time by not going to a random music store and checking out the 5-string basses, for example. Easy.

Faking productivity

This one’s a big one. I realized that I often feel productive just by going on YouTube and watching other people do tasks such as extracting gold from ground-up computer parts, playing heavy metal bass lines, or drawing pretty pictures with Copic markers.

I am not too sure what causes my brain to be tricked so easily when it comes to this, but I at least know about this issue now. Hopefully, I will be able to find psychology texts explaining this effect in greater detail.

Lacking grit

I forgot which YouTube video I was watching when I heard the drummer in the video say something along the lines of “If you’re having a good time while playing drums, you’re not practicing.”

Practice is not just going over easy sections and getting them as fast as possible, or, maybe it is, but practice is definitely also going over the tedious and difficult sections of a piece or a Rubik’s cube solve repeatedly, failing hard in the process, but gradually improving by the rep.

Since I had no grit, I gave up quickly after not having consistent success in solving the 3x3x3 blindfolded. Thus, I stopped practicing, and as a result, got three failed solves (DNFs for “did not finish”) on my scorecard during an official competition in October. If I had more grit, I most likely would’ve forced myself to do more solves, but instead I spent more time chatting, and drastically lowered my chances of even getting a success.

Playing the Numbers Game

Yet, compared to all of the reasons so far, the Numbers Game is a much larger reason for my failure to complete my self-assigned tasks this year.

Here is the way it works:

“Definition”: Let be a task with a label assigned to it, where is a total ordering of labels such that if and only if having label is not more inferior than having label . Then, the Numbers Game is defined as either performing task merely to get as high as possible without actual long-term benefit, or forgetting the original motive behind (which is often deeper than just having a value for ), and getting as high as possible.

Clearly, I’m just nerding out again by throwing out math terms (I have a terrible sense of playfulness), so here are some examples that apply to me:

Example: If I am attempting to achieve higher grades at school even though I do not have use for those higher grades, e.g. I’ll be working in industry, then I am playing the Numbers Game.

Example: If I am dragging myself outside the house, biking 40 minutes for a geocache even though I already know it is a lame lamppost cache that does not take much effort to find, then I am playing the Numbers Game, because I only want my find count to go up even though the original goal was to challenge myself to navigate through more difficult terrain and visit more scenic sites.

Essentially, by playing the Numbers Game, you waste time, and for what? To compare yourself with “potential opposition”, and if anything in life needs to be an axiom, it’s that “comparing the self to others is the best setup for failure”, or even worse, “a false sense of success”.

Unfortunately, it is easy to fall for the Numbers Game, and two things happen:

“Theorem”: For high relative to “potential competition”, the person becomes arrogant and usually starts to slack off, unless that high was obtained as a side-effect of other goals and not directly, in which case the person is not playing the Numbers Game and the “theorem” may or may not hold.

“Theorem”: For low relative to “potential competition”, the person becomes envious of that competition and starts making life decisions merely to boost up their value of , where either boosting up for this person has no long-term benefits, or there are “better things to do” for this person.

Therefore, the Numbers Game might as well equate to eventual regret. For me in particular, the second theorem holds – I could’ve spent more time working on actual side projects rather than making sure every homework assignment was spotless and perfect in presentation. Due to this bad decision, I did not give myself the energy and time needed to write out another blog post all the way until now.

In closing, these are pretty much the reasons I have failed to carry through with my resolutions this year, and hopefully next year I’ll get (an admittedly smaller list of planned things) done.
